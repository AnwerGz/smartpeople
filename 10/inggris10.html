The Great Wallof China is a series of fortifcations made of stone, brick, tamped earth, wood and other materials, generally huilt along an east-to-west line across the historical northemn borders of China to protect the Chinese states and empires against the raids and invasions of the various nomadic groups of the Eurasian Steppe, such as the Mongols, Turkmens, Muns, and Xiongnu people, among others. Several walls were being built as early as the 7th century BC; these were later joined together and made bigger and stronger during the Qin Dynasty (221-206 BC) to protect the northemn borders of the Chinese Empire from invasion by various nomadic groups.

The main Great Wallin e stretches over 13,000 miles (21,196 kilometers) from Shanhaiguan in the east to Lop Lake in the west, along an arc that roughly delineates the southern edge of Inner Mongolia, but stretches to over 20,000 km (12,427 mi) in total when indtuding all of its branches and secondary wall. At its peak, the Ming Wall was guarded by more than one m illion men. It has been estimated that somewhere between 2 and 3 million Chinese died as part of the centuries-long project of building the wall

                                                                              The Owl and the Grasshopper

The Owl always takes her to sleep during the day. Then after sundown, when the rosy light fades from the sky and the shadows rise slowly through the wood, out she comes ruffling and blinking from the old hollow tree. Now her weird "hoo-hoo-hoo-oo-oo" echoes through the quiet wood, and she begins her hunt for the bugs and beetles, frogs and mice she likes so well to eat.

Now there was a certain old Owl who had become very cross and hard to please as she grew older, especially if anything disturbed her daily slumbers. One warm summer afternoon as she dozed away in her den in the old oak tree, a Grasshopper nearby began a joyous but very raspy song. Out popped the old Owl's head from the opening in the tree that served her both for door and for window.

"Get away from here, sir," she said to the Grasshopper. "Have you no manners? You should at least respect my age and leave me to sleep quietly!" But the Grasshopper answered saucily that he had as much right to his place in the sun as the Owl had to her place in the old oak. Then he struck up a louder and still more rasping tune.

The wise old Owl knew quite well that it would do no good to argue with the Grasshopper, nor with anybody else for that matter. Besides, her eyes were not sharp enough by day to permit her to punish the Grasshopper as he deserved. So, she laid aside all the hard words and spoke very kindly to him.

"Well sir," she said, "if I must stay awake, I am going to settle right down to enjoy your singing. Now that I think of it, I have a wonderful wine here, sent to me from Olympus, of which I am told Apollo drinks before he sings to the high gods. Please come up and taste this delicious drink with me. I know it will make you sing like Apollo himself."

The foolish Grasshopper was taken in by the Owl's flattering words. Up he jumped to the Owl's den, but as soon as he was near enough so the old Owl could see him clearly, she pounced upon him and ate him up.

                                                                      THE COCK AND THE FOX STORY

One bright evening as the sun was sinking on a glorious world a wise old Cock flew into a tree to roost. Before he composed himself to rest, he flapped his wings three times and crowed loudly. But just as he was about to put his head under his wing, his beady eyes caught a flash of red and a glimpse of a long-pointed nose, and there just below him stood Master Fox.

"Have you heard the wonderful news?" cried the Fox in a very joyful and excited manner.

"What news?" asked the Cock very calmly. But he had a queer, flattery feeling inside him, for, you know, he was very much afraid of the Fox.

"Your family and mine and all other animals have agreed to forget their differences and live in peace and friendship from now on forever. Just think of it! I simply cannot wait to embrace you! Do come down, dear friend, and let us celebrate the joyful event."

"How grand!" said the Cock. "I certainly am delighted at the news." But he spoke in an absent way, and stretching up on tiptoes, seemed to be looking at something far off.

What is it you see?" asked the Fox a little anxiously.

"Why, it looks to me like a couple of Dogs coming this way. They must have heard the good news and-"

But the Fox did not wait to hear more. Off he started on a run.

"Wait," cried the Cock. "Why do you run? The Dogs are friends of yours now!"

"Yes," answered the Fox. "But they might not have heard the news. Besides, I have a very important errand that I had almost forgotten about."

The Cock smiled as he buried his head in his feathers and went to sleep, for he had succeeded in outwitting a very crafty enemy.

The Fall Of The Berlin Wall

                                                                                                                                                                                                The Berlin Wall, a symbol of the Cold War, divided East and West Berlin from 1961 to 1989. It was constructed by the German Democratic Republic (GDR) to prevent East Germans from fleeing to the West. The Wall stood for 28 years, during which time it became a symbol of the Iron Curtain separating the communist Eastern Bloc and the Western democratic nations.

In the 1980s, the political landscape began to change. Mikhail Gorbachev's policies of Glasnost

(openness) and Perestroika (restructuring) in the Soviet Union prompted reforms throughout Eastern Europe. Public unrest grew in East Germany, with mass protests demanding greater freedoms and

the right to travel.

On November 9, 1989, an East German government official mistakenly announced that the border was open immediately, leading to a surge of East Berliners heading to the Wall. Overwhelmed border guards allowed them to cross. People from both sides began to dismantle the Wall, using whatever tools they could find. The fall of the Berlin Wall marked the beginning of the end for the Soviet Union and led to the reunification of Germany on October 3, 1990.

Wage Rudolf Supratman, often referred to as W.R. Supratman, was a prominent Indonesian songwriter and journalist. He is best known as the composer of the Indonesian national anthem, "Indonesia Raya." His life and achievements have left an indelible mark on the history of Indonesia.

W.R. Supratman was born on March 9, 1903, in Jatinegara, Batavia (now Jakarta), during the Dutch colonial period. He was the seventh child of Senen and Siti Senen. Supratman showed an early interest in music and was encouraged by his older brother, who bought him a violin.

Supratman moved to Makassar (then known as Ujung Pandang) to live with his sister, where he attended school. He further honed his musical skills and began composing music. In addition to his passion for music, Supratman worked as a journalist. He wrote for several publications, using his platform to express his nationalist views and aspirations for Indonesian independence.

The most significant event in Supratman's life was the creation of the song "Indonesia Raya." In 1928, at the Second Indonesian Youth Congress in Jakarta, he introduced "Indonesia Raya" for the first time. The song quickly became a symbol of the Indonesian independence movement. Its powerful lyrics and melody inspired many Indonesians to unite and strive for freedom from colonial rule.

Despite his significant contribution to the national movement, Supratman faced persecution from the Dutch authorities. He was arrested several times for his involvement in the independence movement. Unfortunately, his life was cut short, and he passed away on August 17, 1938, due to illness.

W.R. Supratman's legacy lives on through his music and his role in Indonesia's struggle for independence. "Indonesia Raya" was officially adopted as the national anthem when Indonesia proclaimed its independence on August 17, 1945, exactly seven years after Supratman's death. His contribution to the nation is remembered and celebrated by Indonesians, making him a national hero.

In recognition of his invaluable service to the country, W.R. Supratman is commemorated in various ways, including statues, schools named after him, and his story being taught in Indonesian history classes. His life serves as an inspiration for future generations to continue striving for unity and progress in Indonesia.
